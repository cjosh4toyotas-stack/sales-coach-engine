Deliver It Right · ICE vs BEV
What Changes for a BEV Delivery?
ICE Delivery Covers
Delivery checklist items: vehicle settings, TSS, AMM pairing
Basic functions/features (wipers, mirrors, etc.)
BEV Delivery Adds
Range — State-of-Charge, how range estimates work
Charging — day-to-day access/plan, familiarity with local public charging infrastructure
Key Takeaway
We Already Have a Lot to Cover
Delivery already has a lot to cover no matter the model — BEVs just add more.
Key Takeaway
Higher Volume, Faster Complexity
ICE buyers already understand how to fuel up. BEV Delivery has a much higher volume of information that can get complicated quickly.
Deliver It Right · The Challenge
Why BEV Deliveries Are Harder
What Makes It Difficult
Not enough time to cover everything
Tired buyers get overwhelmed and want to leave ASAP
What Can Go Wrong
Frustration in daily use from not understanding range/charging
Stranded if they run out of charge
Missed appointments or travel delays
The Stakes
Why does an unsuccessful BEV Delivery matter more than an unsuccessful ICE Delivery?
Key Takeaway
  • ICE, HEV, and PHEV buyers can always just go to a gas station if something goes wrong
  • BEV owners need to be ready to go fully electric from Day 1, or they can wind up frustrated or stranded
Deliver It Right · The Solution
Deliver It Right The First Time
C + E
Communication + Education = Success
1st Time
Best Bet: Deliver It Right the First Time
Set expectations prior to Delivery re: time and content to cover
Set up Toyota App and AMM, download the EVgo app during the Delivery appointment
Educate on both home and public charging throughout the sales process — not just at Delivery
Make sure App/AMM connectivity and charging knowledge are squared away before the guest leaves
Don't just do the set-up for them — walk through it together, or they won't learn to use it themselves
If a Guest Is Rushed
Quickly cover the 1-2 most critical charging basics before they leave, even if it means trimming less-essential content.
What "Success" Actually Means
Not "the checklist was technically completed" — success means the guest leaves feeling confident and capable of using their vehicle independently.
Deliver It Right · Set This Expectation
The Charging Reality Check
What Guests Assume
"I'll just plug it into a regular outlet at home and it'll be full by morning."
What's Actually True
  • A standard 120V household outlet (Level 1) typically adds only about 3-5 miles of range per hour
  • Meaningful overnight charging usually requires a Level 2 home charger installation
  • Level 2 installation can take days to weeks — electrician scheduling, a possible panel upgrade, and permitting all take time
This is the single most common source of new-BEV-owner frustration — a guest surprised by this in week one, not at Delivery
Set this expectation explicitly, even if they haven't asked — don't wait for them to discover it the hard way
Deliver It Right · Set This Expectation
Real-World Range vs. the EPA Number
What Meaningfully Reduces Range
Sustained highway speeds
Cold weather
Climate control — especially heat — more so than in a gas vehicle
A Free Tip Worth Sharing
Precondition the cabin (heat or cooling) while still plugged in
That pulls from the grid instead of the battery — meaningful range saved for free
Why This Matters
A guest expecting 281 EPA miles who sees 190 on a cold highway drive with the heat on will think something's wrong with the car.
Set This Expectation
It's not a malfunction — it's physics. Telling them this at Delivery turns a future "is my car broken?" call into a confident "oh right, they told me about this."
Deliver It Right · Set This Expectation
Two Things Guests Don't Expect
🔋
The 12V Battery
Yes, It Can Still Die
BEVs have a small conventional 12V battery for accessories, separate from the traction battery — and it can die just like in any gas car. A quick mention prevents "my electric car has a dead battery?!" confusion later.
🚛
Towing
Flatbed Only — Never Dolly
Most BEVs, especially dual-motor AWD models, require flatbed-only towing. Dolly towing can damage the drivetrain. Guests need to know this before an emergency, not while arguing with a confused tow truck driver at 11pm.

Getting Guests Set Up & Connected
Typical Gas-Vehicle Owner

A New BEV Owner

Ask the guest to download the App while waiting for F&I
Start Delivery with AMM set up already streamlined where possible

Connect & Resources · Easy to Miss
Enroll Every Driver
The Common Gap
Only the person who signed the paperwork gets set up on the Toyota App — but a spouse or family member drives the car regularly too.
Ask This Directly
"Will anyone else be driving this regularly?" If yes, get them enrolled during Delivery too — not as an afterthought weeks later when they're the one stuck without charging tools.
Connect & Resources · Physical Troubleshooting
When Charging Doesn't Start
This is different from an account/app issue — it's a physical charging problem, and guests need to recognize the difference.
Cable not fully seated in the port — the most common fix, and the easiest to overlook
Many public chargers require unlocking or starting the session through the Toyota App before power flows — plugging in alone isn't always enough
Make sure the guest knows this before their first public charging attempt, not after a frustrating one
Connect & Resources · A Selling Point, Too
The Cost-Saving Tip Worth Mentioning
Many utilities offer discounted overnight EV-specific rate plans — often a meaningful savings vs. standard household rates
This costs nothing to mention and directly reinforces "why owning a BEV can be cheaper" — right when the guest is most receptive to hearing it
Encourage guests to check with their local utility provider about EV rate plans or time-of-use discounts

Delivery Practice · Prioritize Under Pressure
Not Everything Is Equally Urgent
Scenario
You have about 10 minutes left and haven't covered charging setup yet. What do you do?
The Right Move
  • Prioritize ruthlessly — cover the ONE thing that prevents them from getting stranded (how to start a charge at home)
  • Comfort features (seat memory, mirror settings) can wait — they're nice-to-know, not need-to-know
  • Give them a clear next step for anything you don't get to
Scenario
A guest says they feel overwhelmed by how much there is to learn.
The Right Move
  • Don't speed up or brush it off — that makes overwhelm worse
  • Acknowledge how they feel, then narrow to the 2-3 things that matter most right now
  • Set a clear, low-pressure plan for covering the rest later
Delivery Practice · When Time Runs Out
Rushing and Skipping Aren't the Same as Finishing
Don't Do This
Rush through remaining content just to technically "cover" it
Quietly skip topics and hope the guest doesn't notice
Tell the guest to "figure it out" or research it themselves
Do This Instead
Proactively find another moment — earlier in the sales process, or a quick follow-up — to finish what's left
Tell the guest directly what you'll cover now vs. later, so they're not left wondering
Remember: success means the guest leaves confident and capable — not just "the appointment ended"
Delivery Practice · Handle It With Confidence
What Guests Actually Ask
Guest Asks
"Why is this taking so much longer than picking up my old car?"
Your Response
  • Acknowledge it honestly — a BEV delivery does take a little more time upfront
  • Frame it as an investment: a few extra minutes now means real confidence for months of ownership, not confusion later
  • Reassure them you're covering exactly what matters most, not padding the appointment
Guest Asks
"What if I forget everything you're telling me once I get home?"
Your Response
  • Completely normal to worry about that — reassure them it's expected, not a sign they're behind
  • Point them to their saved resources: the Delivery Checklist and Toyota App guides they can revisit anytime
  • Let them know the Toyota App and dealership support are there if something doesn't click later

PHEV Fundamentals · The Basics
What Makes a PHEV Different?
🔀
Differentiator 1
Flexible Operation
Capable of standard Hybrid operation or all-electric driving when sufficiently charged — the driver's choice, not a limitation.
🔋
Differentiator 2
Larger Capacity Than a Regular HEV
All-electric range for gas-free short trips and daily commutes — an HEV's small battery can't do this.
🔌
Differentiator 3
Charging Convenience
Still a convenient choice for customers without easy access to a 240V outlet or Level 2 charger at home.
❓
Common Question
PHEV vs. HEV — What's the Real Difference?
A PHEV has a larger battery and can be plugged in for meaningful electric range. An HEV cannot be plugged in at all.
PHEV Fundamentals · Charging
Charging Made as Simple as a Smartphone
The Simple Explanation
Most owners charge at home overnight — "it's like plugging in your phone"
Works on a standard 120V outlet or a 240V/Level 2 charger — no electrical upgrade required for the standard outlet
Charging is optional, not required — the vehicle still runs great as an HEV if it's never plugged in
A Good Comparison Point
On a Level 2 charger, a PHEV charges 10%-80% quicker than a BEV, thanks to its smaller battery capacity
Public Level 2 charging is always an option too — never required, always available
PHEV Fundamentals · The Value Case
The "No Compromise" Solution
The Customer's Worry
"I want to be eco-conscious, but I also take long road trips. Won't I have to choose one or the other?"
Your Answer
No compromise needed. The gas engine is a seamless backup for long road trips — never stranded, never limited by range. Meanwhile, daily errands and commutes can often be handled entirely on all-electric power. Quiet, responsive electric driving during the week; gas-station convenience for the weekend road trip. Best of both worlds.

Match the Explanation to the Customer
The goal isn't to recite everything you know — it's to assess what THIS customer already understands, then meet them there.
SignalWhat It MeansYour Move

Customer Scenarios · Live Practice
Customer Scenario 1
What the Customer Says
"I've been seeing more PHEVs popping up lately, and I'm trying to decide if that's the direction I should go. Some people say they're the 'best of both worlds,' but I'm still wondering if it would just be a hassle."
Potential Question to Ask
"Have you had any experience with charging yet, or would this be your first time exploring that?"
If They Say: "No, I'm still figuring out how charging works"
Low knowledge — "Most owners charge at home overnight, like plugging in your phone. A standard outlet or a 240V/Level 2 charger both work — charging unlocks EV driving, but the vehicle still runs great without it."
Customer Scenarios · Live Practice
Customer Scenario 2
What the Customer Says
"My coworker bought a PHEV about six months ago and hasn't stopped talking about it. Apparently, they've barely been to a gas station since. That got me curious."
Potential Question to Ask
"What are you hoping something like this would do differently from your current vehicle?"
If They Say: "Honestly, I'm mostly hoping it saves me money on gas"
Low knowledge — Discuss potential fuel savings through all-electric range, explain that charging lets the vehicle use electric range first, and position it as a simple way to cut fuel costs without disrupting their current habits.

Myth Busting · The Framework
Good, Better, Best
Not all correct answers are equally effective. Here's the difference, using the "I'll need an electrician" myth as the example:
LevelApproachExample
GoodCorrects the myth with a basic fact"Actually, you can just plug it into a standard 120V outlet."
BetterAdds a Toyota-specific fact or feature"Toyota PHEVs include a dual-voltage charging cable that works with a standard outlet — no upgrades required."
BestConnects the fact to a value proposition"I hear that a lot — but the great thing is it fits the life you already have. Plug into the outlet you already own, and you've basically installed a gas station in your own garage for the night."
Myth Busting · Basic Operation & Infrastructure
Myths About How It Works
Myth
"If the battery dies, I'll be stranded."
Reality
If the high-voltage battery goes low in EV mode, the vehicle automatically switches to HEV mode, using the gas engine to keep going as an efficient hybrid — never stranded. (Separately, the 12V battery can be jump-started like any vehicle if it goes flat.)
Myth
"A Level 2 charger has to be installed in my home."
Reality
Toyota PHEVs can be charged via a common 120V outlet already found in any home. An electrician-installed Level 2, 240V charger is recommended for faster charging — but never required.
Myth Busting · Ownership & Environment
Myths About Cost & Climate
Myth
"The battery needs a $10,000 replacement after 3-5 years"
Reality: the HV battery carries a 10-year/150,000-mile warranty — longer coverage than even the engine or transmission.
Myth
"If I don't plug it in every day, I'll ruin the battery"
Reality: there's no requirement to plug in a PHEV at all. Unplugged, it simply runs as an HEV — no stipulation in the warranty requires charging.
Myth
"Extreme heat or cold kills the battery"
Reality: PHEVs manage battery temperature via a dedicated cooling system. Efficiency differences in extreme weather are about the same as an HEV or gas-only vehicle.
Myth
"I never need to put gas in it if I keep it charged"
Reality: gasoline must stay in the tank. The engine periodically runs even in EV-only use to keep components lubricated and fuel from going stale — the system will prompt for a refill if needed.
